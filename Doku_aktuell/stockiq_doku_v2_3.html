The Learning System
StockIQ is not a static screener. It is a systematic, self-improving investment analysis framework that grows more accurate as it accumulates data, validates its own assumptions, and refines its decision rules through empirical feedback.

1.1 — Philosophy and Design Goals

Most retail investment tools offer either a fixed screener (rules set once and never revisited) or a black-box recommendation engine (rules never disclosed). StockIQ occupies a deliberately different position: a transparent, hypothesis-driven system with explicit factor weights, documented academic sources, and a built-in feedback loop that tests whether its own assumptions hold up over time.

The system draws on five intellectual traditions, each contributing a distinct dimension to the final recommendation:

Core Design Principle

Every factor weight in StockIQ is a hypothesis, not a permanent truth. The system measures its own assumptions through Information Coefficient (IC) analysis and adjusts weights only when empirical evidence justifies the change. No weight is modified based on intuition alone. This discipline separates a learning system from an opinion engine.

The foundation of the learning system is the daily snapshot: a complete cross-section of all 269 tickers with their fundamental data, price momentum, and scoring signals, captured and archived every trading day. This growing time-series is the raw material for factor validation and strategy improvement.

Annual Snapshot Architecture

In addition to daily snapshots, StockIQ maintains an annual snapshot (auto-generated each May 1st ±7 days) covering 2021–2026. Annual data is used for multi-year IC analysis, earnings consistency scoring, and historical backfill of factor values. The annual data reveals longer-term factor decay rates that daily data cannot capture.

1.3 — Factor IC Validation Loop

The Information Coefficient (IC) is the statistical backbone of the learning system. It measures how well a factor value observed today predicts the stock's return over the following 6 months, using Spearman rank correlation (rank-based, robust to outliers).

IC Calculation (Spearman Rank Correlation)
IC = Spearman( factor_value[date_t], return[date_t → date_t+6M] )

Decision thresholds:
  IC > +0.10, |t| > 1.96 → Factor is predictive → Increase weight in scoring
  IC ≈ 0, |t| < 1.96 → No evidence of predictive value → Maintain current weight
  IC < -0.10, |t| > 1.96 → Contrarian signal → Review and potentially reduce weight

The Quality Screening Bias — A Key Discovery

Because StockIQ's universe is pre-selected for high-quality companies (positive FCF, ROCE > 10%, wide moat candidates), traditional fundamental factors such as ROCE and FCF show near-zero IC within this universe. This is statistically expected and does not invalidate the factors. It means: all selected companies are already high-quality, leaving almost no spread for the IC to measure. The real differentiation power within a quality universe comes from momentum (which quality companies are accelerating right now) and EPS revision (which are surprising analysts). This finding directly motivated the introduction of sector-relative normalization and fundamentally different momentum weighting compared to passive factor strategies.

1.4 — Walk-Forward Optimization Loop

Separate from factor IC analysis, StockIQ validates its trading strategy (entry/exit timing, stop-loss rules, market filter) through a rolling walk-forward backtest. The critical principle is that all reported performance metrics are computed exclusively on data the optimizer never saw — eliminating the most common form of backtest fraud.

Why Fundamentals Carry the Highest Weight (35%)

The Fundamentals pillar is the largest single weight, reflecting Buffett's principle: "It is far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price." The FCF/Owner Earnings signal and valuation together account for 60% of the Fundamentals pillar — measuring earning power, cash generation and fair pricing. ROCE carries 20% (reduced from 30% in v5.9.84: IC analysis confirmed ICIR=−0.21 within the quality-screened universe). Momentum (25%) captures near-term price acceleration. Trend (20%) confirms macro price structure. Risk (20%) screens out financially fragile companies. Weights are not permanent: IC analysis governs future adjustments.

Score Filter (SF ≥55) — An Intentional Design Choice

The Score Filter intentionally suppresses SELL signals for fundamentally strong companies. A company with ROCE > 25%, wide economic moat, and composite score 62 that briefly breaks its 200-day MA will receive a muted signal — not an emergency exit. This is aligned with Buffett's documented approach of not exiting high-quality businesses on temporary technical weakness. The SF threshold can be toggled per session in the Synopse and Watchlist views. Walk-forward testing confirmed this filter preserves capital better than pure technical exit rules for quality names.

2.3 — Triple-Confirmation Filter (HC ⭐)

The High-Conviction (HC) filter identifies the highest-quality candidates by requiring three independent signals to align simultaneously. A ticker carrying the ⭐ symbol has passed all three tests — fundamental quality, price momentum, and sector tailwind — in the same period.

Triple-Confirmation Conditions (isHC)
Condition 1 — QUALITY: Composite score cSc() ≥ 65 / 100
Condition 2 — MOMENTUM: 12-month skip-month return > +5% (price acceleration confirmed)
Condition 3 — SECTOR: Sector ETF 12-month return > +10% (macro tailwind confirmed)

HC = true only when ALL THREE conditions are simultaneously satisfied.
HC applies to ANY signal type (BUY, STRONG, PEG-BLK, WATCH, HOLD).

The HC concept draws on the intersection of three well-documented return premia. Quality (Novy-Marx 2013, Fama-French RMW factor): profitable companies outperform over the long run. Price momentum (Jegadeesh & Titman 1993): recent winners continue to outperform for 6–12 months. Sector momentum (Moskowitz & Grinblatt 1999): stocks in leading sectors outperform those in lagging sectors, even controlling for individual stock momentum. The combination of all three is empirically more powerful than any single factor alone.

Reading the HC Counts

On a typical trading day, 18–25 tickers out of 269 carry HC status. This is not the same as the STRONG BUY count. HC applies to all signal types — a PEG-Blocked or WATCH ticker can carry ⭐ if its score, momentum, and sector all qualify. The HC count measures the quality of the opportunity set at any given moment. The Synopse tab shows both counts separately. The Watchlist Synopse comparison bar displays the universe-wide HC count alongside STRONG, BUY, PEG-Block, and SELL totals for immediate cross-tab comparison.

Chapter 03
Momentum Indicators
Momentum is the oldest and most replicated anomaly in finance. Stocks that have outperformed recently tend to continue outperforming — and stocks that have lagged tend to continue lagging — across time horizons of 3 to 12 months. StockIQ implements five distinct momentum signals, each addressing a different facet of price and earnings acceleration, and combines them under a unified VIX-regime dampening framework.
Academic Foundation

Jegadeesh & Titman (1993) first documented cross-sectional price momentum with U.S. data. Asness, Moskowitz & Pedersen (2013) confirmed it in 46 countries across four asset classes. A 2025 meta-study covering 150 years and 46 countries reaffirmed momentum as the most consistent return premium ever documented — outperforming even the value and size factors in long-run predictability (Alpha Architect / Basilico 2025). J.P. Morgan Factor Views (Q2 2026) note momentum as the leading equity factor in Q1 2026, though with its widest winner-loser dispersion since 1990, underscoring the importance of the VIX dampening framework.

3.1 — Skip-Month Price Momentum

The skip-month (or “11-1”) momentum measure is StockIQ’s primary price momentum signal. It is based directly on the original Jegadeesh & Titman (1993) paper, which found that ranking stocks by their 12-month trailing return minus the most recent month — thereby skipping the last month — produces the strongest and most consistent momentum signal.

Skipping the final month is essential. The most recent month’s return exhibits short-term reversal (Jegadeesh 1990): stocks that spike in the final month frequently mean-revert in the next period. Including it would contaminate the momentum signal with noise from market microstructure and bid-ask bounce effects. The skip-month window captures only the durable trend component.

Skip-Month Momentum Formula (StockIQ)
priceMom = (price_now − price_12m_ago) / price_12m_ago × 100    [raw, skip-1]

Normalized to 0–100 score:
priceMomSc = max(0, min(100, 50 + priceMom × 1.5))

Example: +20% 11M return → priceMomSc = 50 + 30 = 80/100
Example: −15% 11M return → priceMomSc = 50 − 22.5 = 27.5/100
Example: 0% (flat) → priceMomSc = 50/100 (neutral)

The score is symmetric around 50. Returns above roughly +33% saturate at 100; returns below −33% floor at 0. This nonlinear clamping prevents extreme outliers (post-crash bouncebacks, meme stocks) from dominating the composite score. fund_juno computes mom12m via 12-month price history, and the dashboard applies the skip-1 adjustment automatically.

Why Not 6-Month Momentum?

The literature (O’Shaughnessy 2011, Quant-Investing QVM Strategy) shows that combining 6-month and 12-month momentum improves signal robustness, as the two windows have low correlation during trend changes. StockIQ currently uses 11M as the primary window. Adding a 6M component (weighted e.g. 50%/50%) is an identified gap for inclusion when fund_juno reaches v8.0 with expanded historical price access. The MACD signal partially captures the shorter-term trend (see 3.2), but a dedicated 6M price window would further strengthen the momentum pillar.

3.2 — MACD Zero-Line Cross Signal

The Moving Average Convergence/Divergence (MACD) indicator serves as both a momentum signal and a key buy/sell trigger in StockIQ. It measures the difference between two exponential moving averages of price, capturing the acceleration and deceleration of trends at a shorter horizon than the skip-month measure.

MACD Parameters (StockIQ Walk-Forward Optimized)
MACD Line    = EMA(12) − EMA(26)
Signal Line = EMA(9) of MACD Line
Histogram   = MACD Line − Signal Line

Buy trigger (ZL-cross): MACD Line crosses above zero from below
Sell trigger: MACD Line crosses below zero
ATR filter: Histogram magnitude > 0.05 × ATR(14) [filters microstructure noise]

MACD Score contribution in momSc():
 With EPS data: MACD weight = 0.45 of momSc()
 Without EPS: MACD weight = 0.50 of momSc()

The zero-line cross (MACD crossing zero, not merely the signal line) was selected after walk-forward analysis across 267 tickers and four 6-month out-of-sample windows. Zero-line crosses are less sensitive to whipsaws than signal-line crosses, producing fewer but higher-quality buy signals. The ATR histogram filter further reduces false positives in low-volatility, range-bound markets.

The MACD is computed by alpha_juno on historical price data (via tk.history()) and used both for the walk-forward signal testing and for live scoring. In the dashboard, the MACD buy trigger is one of two mandatory conditions for a BUY signal (the other being the 200-day MA filter; see Ch.3.5).

3.3 — VIX Regime Dampening

Momentum is the single factor most vulnerable to sudden market regime changes. During high-volatility episodes — financial crises, sudden macro shocks, sharp market reversals — momentum stocks tend to crash violently as crowded positions unwind simultaneously. This is the documented “momentum crash” phenomenon (Daniel & Moskowitz 2016).

J.P. Morgan’s Q2 2026 Factor Monitor notes that the dispersion between momentum winners and losers is currently at its widest since 1990 — a structural warning sign that momentum crash risk is elevated. StockIQ’s VIX dampening framework directly addresses this risk.

VIX Dampening Factor (vixFactor)
VIX < 20    → vixFactor = 1.00  (full momentum signal; calm market)
VIX 20–30 → vixFactor = 0.60  (dampened; elevated uncertainty)
VIX > 30    → vixFactor = 0.20  (strongly suppressed; crisis regime)

momSc() = volAdjScore() × vixFactor

VIX data: fetched via SPY ATM option IV proxy; fallback to ^GSPC implied vol.

The thresholds (20/30) map to widely used risk-regime boundaries in practitioner literature. VIX below 20 represents “normal” market conditions. VIX 20–30 indicates elevated uncertainty (e.g., pre-election, minor credit events). VIX above 30 signals acute stress (e.g., COVID-19 onset: VIX 82; GFC 2008: VIX 89). During crisis regimes, momentum contributes only 20% of its normal weight — effectively muting it while Fundamentals (unchanged at 35%) and Risk (unchanged at 20%) take on relatively more importance.

Walk-Forward Finding: VIX Filter is the Dominant Improvement

Empirical walk-forward testing across four 6-month OOS windows confirmed that the SPY 200-day MA market-phase filter (which correlates closely with VIX regimes) is the single largest performance improvement in the alpha_juno backtester. RSI filters and hysteresis bands showed negligible incremental benefit. This validates the architecture’s emphasis on regime detection over signal complexity.

3.4 — EPS Revision Momentum

Price momentum is driven by information flow. Earnings Per Share (EPS) revision momentum captures a distinct facet: the rate at which analysts revise their forward earnings estimates upward or downward. Upward revisions precede price outperformance; downward revisions precede underperformance — even after controlling for current price momentum (So & Wang 2014).

EPS Revision Score (epsMomSc)
epsGrowth = (eps_ttm − eps_prior_year) / |eps_prior_year|

epsMomSc = max(0, min(100, 50 + epsGrowth × 150))

Contribution to momSc() when available: 10% weight

Positive growth 20% → epsMomSc ≈ 80/100
Negative growth −15% → epsMomSc ≈ 28/100
Not available: excluded; MACD/priceMom share 50%/50%

EPS revision data is fetched from info.get('trailingEps') and info.get('forwardEps') via yfinance. Coverage is approximately 85% of the universe (US stocks: high; European/EM stocks: variable). When EPS data is unavailable, the momentum pillar reverts to a 50/50 MACD/price split, and the AI quality badge reflects the data gap.

3.5 — RSI Overextension Gate

The Relative Strength Index (RSI-14) serves not as a scoring component but as a signal gate: a safety check preventing buy entries into severely overbought territory. The RSI gate implements the empirical finding (Novy-Marx 2012) that returns to momentum strategies are significantly reduced — and crash risk significantly elevated — immediately after prolonged overbought conditions.

RSI Gate Logic
RSI-14: standard Wilder calculation on 14-day closing prices

RSI ≤ 72: BUY / STRONG BUY allowed
RSI > 72: signal downgraded to RSI-BLOCKED (no new entry)

RSI data source: yfinance relativeStrengthIndex14
Fallback: computed from 52-week hi/lo range if direct RSI unavailable

RSI has no weight in the composite score — it is an entry filter only.

The 72 threshold (rather than the conventional 70) was chosen to allow some margin before the overextension zone. Walk-forward testing showed the RSI gate had negligible independent alpha contribution, confirming it functions best as a risk-control overlay rather than a return-generating signal. It prevents entries at local peaks while not blocking legitimate strong-momentum stocks in early trend stages.

Momentum Pillar: Composite Assembly

The five signals combine into a single momSc() value as follows: MACD contributes the entry/exit trigger character and short-term trend direction; price momentum (skip-month) provides the 11-month trend signal; EPS revision adds earnings quality to price movement; VIX dampening is the regime overlay applied to the entire pillar; RSI is a pass/fail gate applied downstream at signal generation. This layered architecture ensures that momentum is confirmed from multiple independent directions before a BUY is issued.

Fundamental Indicators
Fundamentals carry the largest weight in StockIQ (35%) because business quality is the most durable return driver. Where momentum can crash in days and trend can reverse in weeks, a company with high returns on capital, strong free cash flow, and low debt compounds quietly and persistently. This chapter documents all nine fundamental signals — their academic derivation, StockIQ formula, parameter rationale, and known data limitations.
Buffett (2024): EBITDA is Flawed — EBIT is Honest

In his 2024 annual letter, Warren Buffett described EBITDA as “a flawed favorite of Wall Street.” His critique: adding back depreciation and amortization creates a misleading picture of earnings power, because real assets wear out and require capital expenditure to maintain. StockIQ’s valuation framework reflects this: EV/EBIT (which keeps D&A as a real cost) is given equal or greater weight than EV/EBITDA in the dual-signal valSc() computation. Owner Earnings (Buffett 1986) go further, explicitly adjusting for growth capex vs. maintenance capex.

4.1 — ROCE: Return on Capital Employed

ROCE is a cornerstone of the Fundamentals pillar, carrying 20% weight within fSc() (reduced from 30% in v5.9.84 following IC analysis: ROCE ICIR=−0.21 within the quality-screened universe). It measures how efficiently a company generates profit from the capital committed to the business — both equity and debt. Unlike Return on Equity (ROE), ROCE is not inflated by financial leverage: a company that borrows heavily to boost ROE will not score well on ROCE. This makes ROCE a purer measure of operational quality.

ROCE Formula & Scoring
ROCE = EBIT / Capital Employed
Capital Employed = Total Assets − Current Liabilities

Normalized score (roceSc):
ROCE ≥ 25% → score 90–100  (exceptional; wide moat territory)
ROCE 15–25% → score 65–89  (strong quality)
ROCE 8–15% → score 40–64  (average; cost of capital roughly covered)
ROCE < 8% → score 0–39   (value destruction risk)

yfinance source: income_stmt EBIT + balance_sheet totalAssets − currentLiabilities
IQR outlier filter: values outside Q1−1.5×IQR / Q3+1.5×IQR capped in historical data

A ROCE of 25% or higher — Buffett’s informal threshold for a “wonderful business” — indicates that the company earns 25 cents of pre-tax operating profit for every dollar of capital deployed. Companies sustaining ROCE > 25% over multiple years typically possess durable competitive advantages (economic moats). The StockIQ universe is pre-screened for quality, which creates a Quality-Screening-Bias: within the universe, ROCE differences are compressed, which has been observed to reduce within-universe factor IC (see Ch.9).

GOOGL Case Study: IQR Filter in Action

Historical backfill of Alphabet (GOOGL) produced a ROCE value of 79.8% for 2021 — plausible given GOOGL’s capital-light advertising model, but an extreme outlier within the universe. The IQR filter detected this as outside the Q3+1.5×IQR boundary and capped it at the 97th percentile of the annual cross-section. This prevented artificial score inflation in historical IC analysis. IQR filtering is now mandatory for all annual snapshot data.

4.2 — FCF Yield: Free Cash Flow Yield

Free Cash Flow Yield measures how much cash profit a company generates relative to its market value, after all capital expenditures required to maintain and grow the business. It is the “what you actually get as an owner” metric — unaffected by accounting choices around depreciation, amortization, or revenue recognition. FCF Yield carries 30% weight within fSc(), as the combined FCF/OE signal: max(fcfSc, oeSc) uses whichever of FCF Yield or Owner Earnings Yield scores higher for each company, providing a more complete picture of earnings power (v5.9.84, IC=+0.155***).

FCF Yield Formula & Scoring
FCF = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditures
FCF Yield = FCF / Market Capitalization

Score mapping:
FCF Yield ≥ 8% → score 90–100  (deeply undervalued cash generator)
FCF Yield 4–8% → score 65–89  (healthy; typical quality stock)
FCF Yield 1–4% → score 35–64  (fair; or growth premium priced in)
FCF Yield < 0% → score 0–20   (burning cash; high risk)

yfinance: operatingCashflow − capitalExpenditures / marketCap
Sanity gate: FCF Yield > 40% or < −100% → set to None (data error)
NVO Case Study: FCF Collapse as Strategic Signal

Novo Nordisk (NVO) recorded a dramatic FCF Yield drop from 30.8% to approximately 0.2% across 2023–2024. A naïve scoring system would have issued a SELL signal. StockIQ’s interpretation, supported by Owner Earnings analysis (4.3), is different: NVO’s FCF collapsed because of strategic growth capex — capacity expansion for Ozempic and semaglutide products. This is not quality deterioration; it is capital deployment into a dominant market position. The NVO case motivated the addition of Owner Earnings as a complement to FCF Yield, providing a signal less distorted by growth-phase capex cycles.

4.3 — Owner Earnings Yield

Owner Earnings was defined by Warren Buffett in the 1986 Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter: “these represent (a) reported earnings plus (b) depreciation, depletion, amortization, and certain other non-cash charges… less (c) the average annual amount of capitalized expenditures for plant and equipment that the business requires to fully maintain its long-term competitive position and its unit volume.”

Unlike FCF, Owner Earnings separates maintenance capex (required to stay competitive) from growth capex (elective investment in expansion). A company like NVO with massive growth capex will show suppressed FCF but healthy Owner Earnings if its maintenance capex needs are modest. Owner Earnings is the closest quantitative approximation to “what the business would earn if it stopped growing.”

Owner Earnings Yield (StockIQ Display Field)
Owner Earnings = Net Income + Depreciation & Amortization − Total CapEx
Owner Earnings Yield = Owner Earnings / Market Capitalization

Note: StockIQ uses Total CapEx as a conservative approximation.
A refined version would use maintenance CapEx only (estimated as % of PP&E).

Status: Integrated into fSc() since v5.9.84 via max(fcfSc, oeSc). IC=+0.155*** (t=4.19, n=717, Mai 2026).
Roadmap: IC testing after 30 snapshots to assess predictive power vs. FCF Yield.

Owner Earnings Yield is currently shown on the Fundamentals tab as an informational field alongside FCF Yield. The dual display allows the investor to compare them: large divergence (FCF much lower than OE Yield) typically signals heavy growth-phase investment; convergence suggests mature, cash-generative operations.

4.4 — EV/EBIT: Enterprise Value Multiple

EV/EBIT is StockIQ’s primary valuation multiple, capturing how much the market charges per dollar of operating profit. It corrects for capital structure differences (unlike P/E, which is affected by leverage) and uses EBIT rather than EBITDA — honouring Buffett’s principle that depreciation is a real economic cost that should not be added back.

EV/EBIT Score (evEbitSc)
EV = Market Cap + Total Debt − Cash
EV/EBIT = EV / EBIT

Score mapping (inverted: lower multiple = better):
EV/EBIT ≤ 10 → score 90–100  (deep value)
EV/EBIT 10–18 → score 60–89  (fair to reasonable)
EV/EBIT 18–30 → score 30–59  (expensive; growth priced in)
EV/EBIT > 30 → score 0–29   (expensive or EBIT near zero)
EV/EBIT < 0 (EBIT negative) → score = 0

4.5 — Sector-Relative PEG Ratio

The PEG ratio (Price/Earnings to Growth) was popularized by Peter Lynch as a way to adjust P/E for the growth rate being paid for. A PEG below 1.0 is classically considered “cheap relative to growth.” However, raw PEG comparisons across sectors are misleading: a pharma company growing at 8% and a technology company growing at 25% will both produce a PEG around 1.5–2.0, but mean completely different things within their sectors.

Sector-Relative PEG (pegRelSc)
PEG = (P/E Ratio) / (EPS Growth Rate × 100)

Sector Median PEG computed from all tickers in same GICS sector in universe.

Relative PEG = ticker PEG / sector_median_PEG

Score:
Relative PEG ≤ 0.60 → score 90–100  (40% cheaper than sector peers)
Relative PEG 0.60–0.90 → score 65–89
Relative PEG 0.90–1.20 → score 40–64  (roughly in line with sector)
Relative PEG > 1.50 → score < 30   (materially expensive vs. peers)
PEG ≥ 3.0 → BUY signal blocked (PEG-BLOCKED signal)

IC Analysis finding: Raw PEG IC = near-neutral (quality-screening bias).
Sector-relative normalization is the architectural response.

The sector-relative approach was motivated by the IC Analysis finding in May 2026 (see Ch.9): all fundamental factors showed near-neutral IC within the StockIQ universe because the universe had already been pre-screened for quality. Sector-relative normalization restores cross-sectional differentiation by comparing each ticker to its own peer group, not to the entire universe.

4.6 — Piotroski F-Score Quality Gate

The Piotroski F-Score (Piotroski 2000) is a nine-point accounting-based quality score that identifies financially sound companies within a value universe. Each of the nine criteria is binary (0 or 1), testing profitability, leverage, liquidity, and operational efficiency. A score of 7–9 indicates strong financial health; 0–2 signals financial distress.

Piotroski F-Score: 9 Criteria
PROFITABILITY (4 criteria):
F1. ROA > 0 (positive return on assets)
F2. Operating Cash Flow > 0
F3. ROA increasing YoY
F4. OCF/Assets > ROA (accruals quality check)

LEVERAGE & LIQUIDITY (3 criteria):
F5. Debt ratio decreasing YoY
F6. Current ratio increasing YoY
F7. No new share issuance in past year

OPERATIONAL EFFICIENCY (2 criteria):
F8. Gross margin increasing YoY
F9. Asset turnover ratio increasing YoY

StockIQ Piotroski Penalty:
pioScore < 2 AND ≥2 criteria measurable → fSc() × 0.85 (15% reduction)

In StockIQ, Piotroski is used as a negative quality gate rather than a positive scoring component. Companies with very low Piotroski scores (< 2 out of those that can be measured) receive a 15% penalty on their entire Fundamental score. This asymmetric design reflects empirical evidence: Piotroski works best as a distress screen, not as a ranking device within a quality-filtered universe (where most stocks already score 5–8).

4.7 — EVAR: Earnings Variability Score

EVAR (Earnings Variability) is a risk signal that measures the consistency of reported earnings over time. It is inspired by the MSCI Quality Factor’s “earnings variability” descriptor, which penalizes companies with erratic, unpredictable earnings. Low EVAR = earnings are stable and predictable; high EVAR = earnings are volatile and unreliable.

EVAR Calculation (StockIQ)
EVAR = Standard deviation of annual EPS over last 4 years / |mean EPS|

Sector-relative EVAR = ticker EVAR / sector_median_EVAR

Score (inverted: lower variability = higher score):
Relative EVAR ≤ 0.5 → evarRelSc ≈ 85–100  (very stable; half sector volatility)
Relative EVAR ≈ 1.0 → evarRelSc ≈ 50     (in line with sector peers)
Relative EVAR ≥ 2.0 → evarRelSc ≈ 10–20  (twice sector volatility)

EVAR weight in rSc: 10% (Risk pillar component)

EVAR is computed from annual EPS history in the snapshot data. Companies in cyclical sectors (Energy, Financials, Materials) will naturally show higher EVAR; the sector-relative normalization adjusts for this, so a cyclical company with stable-for-its-sector earnings can score well on EVAR. Sector medians are recomputed at each snapshot run.

4.8 — Earnings Consistency (Buffett Consistency Test)

The Earnings Consistency Score measures whether a company’s ROCE has been stable and persistently high over multiple years — the “durable competitive advantage” test. Buffett repeatedly emphasizes that a single year of high returns means little; it is the persistence of high ROCE that signals a genuine economic moat.

Consistency Score (consScore)
Inputs: ROCE values from annual snapshots (2021–present)

IQR-filtered ROCE std-dev = std-dev after removing outliers beyond Q1−1.5×IQR / Q3+1.5×IQR

Consistency = 1 − (IQR-filtered std-dev / mean ROCE)
max(0, min(1, Consistency)) → normalized to 0–100 score

Weight in fSc(): 5% (smallest component; grows as snapshots accumulate)

Minimum data requirement: 3 annual snapshots (2023, 2024, 2025 minimum)
Status (May 2026): ~17 daily snapshots available; annual data backfilled to 2021.

The IQR filter is essential for historical integrity: without it, a single anomalous year (acquisition write-off, pandemic distortion) dominates the standard deviation calculation, producing misleading consistency scores. After filtering, a company that consistently earns 22–26% ROCE across five years will score near 100 on consistency; a company that earned 35% one year and 4% the next will score near 0.

Risk Indicators & Sector Signals
Risk is not a single number. StockIQ measures it across three independent dimensions: market sensitivity (Beta), earnings predictability (EVAR), and macro regime (VIX). Together they form the Risk pillar, contributing 20% to the composite score.

5.1 — Beta: Market Sensitivity

Beta measures how much a stock amplifies or dampens overall market movements. A Beta of 1.5 means the stock typically moves 50% more than the market in either direction. Beta is sourced directly from yfinance (info.get('beta')) and reflects the trailing 5-year weekly regression against the S&P 500.

Beta Score (betaSc)

Beta 0.5–0.8 → score 72–89
Beta 0.8–1.2 → score 50–71  (market-like; neutral)

Beta’s 40% weight in the Risk pillar reflects empirical evidence of the low-volatility anomaly: over long horizons, low-beta stocks persistently outperform high-beta stocks on a risk-adjusted basis (Baker, Bradley & Wurgler 2011). This is counterintuitive — the CAPM predicts higher beta should earn higher returns — but has been confirmed across 21 countries and decades of data. Walk-forward IC analysis (May 2026) showed beta IC = −0.028 pooled, strongly regime-dependent (−0.53 in bear markets, +0.26 in bull markets). Beta therefore works best as a risk control rather than a return predictor.

5.2 — EVAR: Earnings Variability (Sector-Relative)

While Beta measures price volatility against the market, EVAR measures earnings quality: how predictable and consistent a company’s earnings are over time. Highly variable earnings are a symptom of either cyclical exposure, management quality issues, or structural business fragility.

Sector-relative: EVAR_rel = EVAR_ticker / EVAR_sector_median

The sector-relative normalization is critical for EVAR’s fairness. Cyclical sectors (Energy, Metals, Autos) structurally exhibit high EPS variability; penalizing them on an absolute basis would systematically underweight entire sectors regardless of individual company quality. MSCI’s Quality Factor uses an equivalent measure (“earnings variability” descriptor) in the same sector-relative manner.

5.4 — Sector ETF Momentum Signal

Sector momentum — measuring whether an entire sector is in a price uptrend — provides a macro tailwind filter distinct from individual stock momentum. Moskowitz & Grinblatt (1999) showed that sector momentum explains a significant portion of individual stock momentum returns, and sector leaders disproportionately contribute to momentum premia.

This reflects Moskowitz & Grinblatt’s finding that the sector tailwind effect is primarily a threshold phenomenon: being in a winning sector matters; the degree of winning matters less.

WL3 is the empirical validation layer for signal quality. The horizon bucket analysis (7–14d, 14–21d, 21–30d) allows the data to reveal at which horizon the score-return relationship is strongest — without artificial gatekeeping. The Confidence Indicator makes data reliability transparent, replacing the blunt 30-day rule.

IC Feedback Loop — Empirical Results
The IC Feedback Loop is StockIQ's mechanism for self-improvement. This chapter documents the complete methodology, the May 2026 empirical results, and their concrete consequences for the scoring model.

8.1 — Methodology: Annual IC Analysis

Factor IC analysis correlates each factor’s value observed at year-end with the subsequent full-year return. The Spearman rank correlation is used (robust to outliers). Four annual time pairs are available: 2021→22, 2022→23, 2023→24, 2024→25.

Annual IC Analysis (stockiq_annual_ic_v1_2.py)
IC(factor, year) = Spearman( factor_values[Dec.year], returns[year → year+1] )

IC-IR = mean(IC_2122, IC_2223, IC_2324, IC_2425) / std(IC years)
 → Measures stability of predictive signal across market cycles

Significance: |t| = IC × √((n−2)/(1−IC²)) > 1.96 → p < 0.05

Confidence levels:
 n ≥ 400: IC = 0.10 detectable
 n ≥ 150: IC = 0.15 detectable
 n ≥ 30: IC = 0.25 detectable

8.2 — May 2026 IC Results: Key Findings

Finding 1: FCF is the Most Stable Predictor

FCF Yield pooled IC = +0.062 (not individually significant, t=1.71), but IC-IR = +1.39 — the highest stability of all measured factors. This means FCF predicted returns consistently in the same positive direction across all three measurable years. IC-IR > 1.0 is considered a high-quality signal in institutional factor research. The FCF weight of 30% in fSc() is confirmed appropriate.

Finding 2: Debt/Equity IC is Inverted — Financial Sector Bias

Debt (D/E ratio) IC = +0.103, significant (t=2.76, IC-IR=1.11) — but in the wrong direction. Higher debt predicted better returns. Root cause: financial companies (banks, insurers) have structurally high D/E ratios (>300%) and strongly outperformed in 2022–2025. The debtSc() function was incorrectly penalizing financial companies for a structural accounting feature, not operational risk. Action taken: debtSc() in v5.9.78 now returns a neutral score of 65 for financial-sector tickers.

Finding 3: Quality-Screening Bias Confirmed

ROCE IC = −0.063 (not significant, t=−1.76). This is not a failure of ROCE as a quality metric — it is a statistical consequence of the universe design. All 269 StockIQ tickers are pre-screened for ROCE > 10%, positive FCF, and wide-moat characteristics. Within this already-high-quality universe, ROCE has almost no cross-sectional spread to differentiate. The IC measuring instrument cannot detect signal in the absence of variation. ROCE remains the correct inclusion criterion for the universe; it is not the appropriate ranking criterion within the universe.

8.3 — Weight Adjustment Formula

The IC-IR provides the basis for systematic weight adjustments. The adjustment formula prevents overreaction to a single year of data while gradually moving weights toward empirically validated factors.

Weight Adjustment Formula (w_adj)
w_adj = w_base × (1 + 0.15 × clamp(IC-IR, −1, +1))

Examples:
FCF: IC-IR = +1.39 → w_adj = 0.30 × 1.15 = 0.345  (+15% increase)
Debt: IC-IR = +1.11 (inverted) → architectural fix applied, formula secondary
ROCE: IC-IR = −0.29 → w_adj = 0.30 × 0.957 = 0.287  (minor reduction)

Principle: adjustments are capped at ±15% per IC cycle to prevent regime-overfitting.
Minimum IC-IR threshold for adjustment: |IC-IR| ≥ 0.5 AND significance p < 0.05.

Weight adjustments from the May 2026 IC cycle are not yet implemented pending the annual_backfill v1.3 completion (to give mom_skip, evar, and ev_ebit their first measurable IC). The architectural fix (debtSc sector-aware) was implemented immediately because the direction inversion was unambiguous and the root cause identified. For factors where IC is ambiguous or near-zero, no changes are made — consistent with the principle that the walk-forward is the empirical arbiter.

9.3 — Earnings Consistency Score

The annual archive directly powers the Consistency Score — the 5% component of fSc() that measures multi-year ROCE stability. This is Buffett’s “durable competitive advantage” test: a company with persistently high ROCE (say, 20–25% across five years) has a deeper moat than one that earned 40% once and 5% the next.

Consistency Score Algorithm
Inputs: ROCE values from annual snapshots (minimum 3 years)

Step 1: IQR Outlier Filter
  Q1 = 25th percentile of ROCE values
  Q3 = 75th percentile
  IQR = Q3 − Q1
  Remove values outside [Q1 − 1.5×IQR, Q3 + 1.5×IQR]

Step 2: Consistency Score
  std_filtered = std-dev of filtered ROCE values
  Consistency = 1 − (std_filtered / max(1, mean_ROCE))
  consScore = max(0, min(100, Consistency × 100))

Case Study — GOOGL 2026 Backfill:
  ROCE 2023 = 79.8% (IQR outlier → filtered out)
  Remaining values consistent → consScore correctly elevated

CHAPTER 10 — SHILLER CAPE & MACRO ALLOCATION StockIQ v5.9.78
Chapter 10
Shiller CAPE & Macro Allocation
The Cyclically Adjusted Price-to-Earnings ratio (CAPE) is the most academically validated long-horizon valuation metric. It does not predict short-term returns, but it is one of the most reliable predictors of 10-year equity returns. StockIQ uses it as a macro allocation signal, not as a scoring component.

10.1 — CAPE Methodology

Nobel laureate Robert Shiller’s CAPE ratio divides the current market price by the average of the previous ten years of earnings, adjusted for inflation. Ten-year smoothing eliminates the noise of business cycle peaks and troughs, revealing the “true” cyclically adjusted earnings power of the market.

CAPE (Shiller P/E Ratio)
CAPE = Current Market Price / Average(Real EPS, 10 years)

Historical S&P 500 CAPE context:
CAPE < 15: Deeply undervalued. Historically: 10Y forward returns > 12% p.a.
CAPE 15–20: Fair value range. Balanced opportunity.
CAPE 20–25: Moderately elevated. Tempered expectations.
CAPE 25–35: Stretched. Historical: 10Y forward returns ≈ 3–6%.
CAPE > 35: Extreme. Very low or negative real 10Y forward returns historically.

(Note: CAPE above 20 has been the norm since 1990s. Simple thresholds require
era-adjustment. Compare vs. own historical distribution, not absolute levels.)

10.2 — Why CAPE is NOT in the Score

The architectural decision to exclude CAPE from the composite score was deliberate and carefully evaluated. CAPE has zero predictive power at horizons below 3 years — it is useless for timing individual entries or exits within a 6-to-18 month signal horizon. Including it in the score would systematically penalize equity signals during extended high-valuation periods (e.g., 2013–2021 where CAPE was consistently above 25 yet equities returned 15%+ annually).

Architectural Decision: CAPE as Macro Display Signal Only

CAPE is displayed in the Allocation Tab as a reference indicator for the 60%/20%/15%/5% target allocation. A sustained CAPE above 35 is a signal to consider maintaining the higher end of the cash reserve and being selective with new position sizing — not to suppress BUY signals or lower composite scores. The stock-picking layer (StockIQ score) operates independently of macro valuation because stock-specific quality and momentum can generate alpha even in overvalued markets.

10.3 — norm_pe: EPS Smoothing for Individual Stocks

At the individual stock level, a CAPE-equivalent normalization can be implemented by substituting the trailing TTM EPS with a 5-year average EPS in the P/E calculation. This “norm_pe” eliminates single-year distortions (write-offs, windfall gains, pandemic spikes) from the valuation signal.

Normalized P/E (norm_pe — Planned)
norm_pe = Current Price / avg(EPS_t, EPS_{t-1}, EPS_{t-2}, EPS_{t-3}, EPS_{t-4})

vs. regular P/E = Current Price / EPS_ttm

Use case: replaces raw P/E in PEG denominator to smooth growth calculation
Status (May 2026): Planned for inclusion in valSc() after annual_backfill v1.3
Prerequisite: 5 years of annual EPS data per ticker in annual snapshot archive
